ABSTRACT
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Fluoride contamination of drinking water is a major public-health problem, and
adsorption onto low-cost biosorbents such as coconut-husk activated carbon (CHAC)
is a practical remediation route. Predicting fluoride uptake (q, mg/g) from
operating conditions is difficult because classical isotherm theory captures the
dominant chemistry but leaves systematic, condition-dependent errors. This work
develops, implements, and validates a HYBRID physics-informed machine-learning
framework in which a mechanistic multi-factor Langmuir model supplies a chemistry
baseline and a supervised stacked ensemble predicts the baseline's residuals, so
the two combine into a corrected prediction
(q_hybrid = q_Langmuir + q_ML_correction).

A 500-run experimental matrix over 10 physicochemical factors was generated by
Latin Hypercube Sampling (LHS) and labelled with a physics-based adsorption
simulator (Langmuir isotherm modulated by an Arrhenius temperature correction, a
Gaussian pH response, pseudo-first/second-order kinetics, and literature-bounded
ion-competition and fouling penalties). A degree-2 polynomial Langmuir regression
over the 10 factors (66 expanded terms) achieved R2 = 0.816 and RMSE = 0.528 mg/g.
Residual diagnostics showed the 18.4% unexplained variance is not random noise but
a structured, pH-driven signal: the baseline under-predicts by +0.649 mg/g in the
optimal pH 6.5-7 window and over-predicts by -0.525 mg/g in the acidic pH 4-5
window. A 38-feature engineered representation -- augmented with 14
physics-mirroring terms -- exposed this non-linearity. Four residual learners were
trained with 5-fold cross-validation; XGBoost and a stacking ensemble
(XGBoost + Random Forest + Gradient Boosting, Ridge meta-learner) both recovered
R2 = 0.68 of the residual variance on out-of-fold data. Integrating the residual
model with the Langmuir baseline produced a hybrid predictor with a 5-fold
cross-validated R2 = 0.942 and RMSE = 0.297 mg/g -- meeting the project target of
R2 >= 0.94 and reducing prediction error by 43.7% relative to chemistry alone,
while keeping every stage interpretable. The contribution is a complete,
reproducible, and explainable pipeline demonstrating that physics-ML
hybridisation converts an 81.6% chemistry baseline into a >94% predictor.

KEYWORDS: fluoride removal; activated carbon; Langmuir isotherm; physics-informed
machine learning; residual learning; Latin Hypercube Sampling; XGBoost; stacking
ensemble; feature engineering; water treatment.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
1. INTRODUCTION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
1.1  Problem context. Chronic ingestion of fluoride above the WHO guideline of
1.5 mg/L causes dental and skeletal fluorosis and affects millions of people in
endemic regions. Adsorption is favoured for decentralised treatment because it is
low-cost, simple, and compatible with agricultural-waste sorbents such as
coconut-husk activated carbon (CHAC).

1.2  Modelling gap. Classical adsorption design relies on equilibrium isotherms
(Langmuir, Freundlich) and kinetic models (pseudo-first/second-order). These are
mechanistic and interpretable but are usually fitted under single-variable
conditions and degrade when many operating variables and real-water interferents
(competing ions, hardness, natural organic matter) act simultaneously. Purely
data-driven machine learning fits such multi-factor data but is opaque,
data-hungry, and can extrapolate unphysically.

1.3  Proposed approach (the CS contribution). We adopt a HYBRID, physics-informed
strategy. A mechanistic Langmuir model provides the bulk of the signal and
guarantees physically reasonable behaviour; a machine-learning model is trained
only on what the physics misses (the residuals). The final predictor is

        q_hybrid = q_Langmuir(x)  +  f_ML(phi(x))

where x is the 10-factor operating vector and phi(x) is an engineered feature map.
This keeps the model interpretable -- the physics term dominates and is analysable
-- while letting ML absorb the structured non-linearities a quadratic polynomial
cannot represent.

1.4  Objectives.
  (O1) Build a space-filling 10-factor experimental design and a physics-based
       labelling simulator for the response q (mg/g).
  (O2) Fit and validate an interpretable multi-factor Langmuir baseline.
  (O3) Prove that the baseline residuals are learnable (structured, not noise) and
       engineer features that expose that structure.
  (O4) Train and compare residual learners (Random Forest, XGBoost, Gradient
       Boosting, MLP) under cross-validation and integrate the best into a hybrid.
  (O5) Show the hybrid reaches R2 >= 0.94 with quantified, interpretable gains.

1.5  Contributions.
  - A reproducible (seed-fixed) physics-ML pipeline for fluoride-adsorption
    prediction over 10 simultaneously varied factors.
  - Evidence that Langmuir residuals carry a recoverable pH-driven signal, with a
    quantified residual-vs-pH diagnostic.
  - A 38-feature engineered representation (plus 14 physics-mirroring features)
    that breaks the OLS zero-correlation barrier and makes residuals learnable.
  - A validated hybrid predictor: 5-fold cross-validated R2 = 0.942,
    RMSE = 0.297 mg/g, a 43.7% error reduction over the chemistry baseline.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
2. RELATED WORK
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
2.1  Isotherm and kinetic modelling. The Langmuir isotherm (monolayer adsorption
on a homogeneous surface, no lateral interaction) remains the workhorse for
adsorption and routinely achieves high R2 on single-variable equilibrium data;
comparative studies report Langmuir fits with R2 > 0.99 for activated-carbon
systems and good fits (R2 > 0.9) for fluoride uptake specifically [3,6].

2.2  Machine learning for adsorption. Recent work applies generalized linear
models, decision trees, Random Forest, gradient-boosted trees, SVM and multilayer
perceptrons to predict adsorption capacity across adsorbents and adsorbates,
generally reporting strong accuracy but limited mechanistic interpretability [4].

2.3  Physics-informed / hybrid modelling. Coupling mechanistic models with ML
residual correction (a "grey-box" or residual-learning strategy) retains
interpretability and physical plausibility while recovering accuracy lost to model
misspecification. This paper instantiates that strategy end-to-end for fluoride
adsorption on coconut-husk activated carbon. The project's internal literature
survey covers 40+ papers spanning 1918-2025 (see docs/PROJECT_REFERENCES.md).

3. MATERIALS AND METHODS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
3.1  Pipeline overview.
The computational pipeline is a staged data flow (Figure 1):

   Phase 1   ->  LHS design (500 x 10)  +  physics simulator  ->  labelled data
   Phase 2   ->  multi-factor Langmuir regression  ->  predictions + residuals
   Phase 3   ->  residual diagnostics + feature engineering  ->  ML-ready tables
   Phase 4   ->  residual learners (RF / XGBoost / GBT / MLP), 5-fold CV + tuning
   Phase 5   ->  hybrid integration q_hybrid and cross-validated validation

[FIGURE 1: Five-phase architecture of the hybrid physics-ML workflow, annotated
with the R2 achieved at each modelling stage. -> images/fig1_pipeline.png]

3.2  Factor selection and ranges (Phase 1).
Ten factors span the operational and water-chemistry space: pH, initial fluoride
concentration C0, contact time, adsorbent dose, temperature, flow rate, chloride,
hardness (Ca/Mg as CaCO3), carbonate/bicarbonate, and natural organic matter
(NOM). Each maps to a distinct physicochemical mechanism (surface charge and F-
speciation; isotherm driving force; kinetics; available surface area; Arrhenius
effects; transport/residence time; and four competition/fouling interferents).

TABLE 1. Experimental factors and ranges (Latin Hypercube design space).
  Factor                     Unit         Min     Max
  pH                         -            3       9
  Initial conc. C0           mg/L         1       10
  Contact time               min          10      120
  Adsorbent dose             g/L          0.5     5
  Temperature                deg C        20      40
  Flow rate                  L/min        0.5     2.0
  Chloride                   mg/L         0       100
  Hardness (as CaCO3)        mg/L         0       500
  Carbonate / bicarbonate    mg/L         0       100
  NOM                        mg/L         0       50

3.3  Design of experiments (Phase 1).
Latin Hypercube Sampling (N = 500, k = 10, uniform marginals, seed = 42) was
chosen over full factorial (2^10 = 1024 runs, corners only) and naive random
sampling for its space-filling, low-collinearity coverage of the 10-dimensional
space. Verification confirmed near-ideal uniform marginals on every factor
(Figure 2).

3.4  Physics-based response simulator (Phase 1).
Because a 500-run wet-lab campaign over 10 factors is infeasible, q (mg/g) was
generated by a mechanistic simulator:

   q = qmax * KL_T * Ce / (1 + KL_T * Ce)
         * pH_factor * kinetic_factor * ion_penalty * NOM_penalty + epsilon

   KL_T        = KL_ref * exp(-Ea/R * (1/T - 1/T_ref))      (Arrhenius)
   pH_factor   = exp(-((pH - 6.5)^2 / (2 * 1.5^2)))          (Gaussian, peak 6.5)
   Ce          = C0 * exp(-k * Dose * Time / 60)             (concentration decay)
   kinetic     = 1 - exp(-k2 * Dose * Time)                  (PSO uptake)
   ion_penalty = 1 - min(0.30, sum of Cl/hardness/carbonate effects)
   NOM_penalty = 1 - 0.15 * NOM/50
   epsilon     = N(0, 0.05) * q_base                         (5% measurement noise)

Parameters (literature, CHAC): qmax = 8.5 mg/g, KL_ref = 0.12 L/mg at 25 C,
Ea = 20 kJ/mol, optimal pH 6.5, Gaussian width 1.5 pH units. A logarithmic-
transform bug producing unphysical near-zero values was found and corrected during
QA; the corrected dataset spans q = 1.42-8.32 mg/g (mean 4.10, std 1.23), with pH
the strongest single correlate (r = +0.27).

[FIGURE 2: Exploratory data analysis -- the 10 (uniform) factor distributions, the
q_removal response histogram, and the q-vs-pH relationship coloured by dose, with
the Gaussian peak at pH 6.5 visible. -> images/fig2_eda.png]

3.5  Multi-factor Langmuir baseline (Phase 2).
The chemistry baseline is a degree-2 polynomial regression over the 10
standardised factors: 10 linear + 10 squared + 45 pairwise interaction = 66 terms,
fitted by Ordinary Least Squares on all 500 points. Both X and y were
standardised; predictions were inverse-transformed for reporting. The residual
r = q_actual - q_Langmuir is the learning target for the ML stage.

3.6  Residual diagnostics and feature engineering (Phase 3).
Residuals were analysed for structure by binning on each factor and by
correlation. Because OLS guarantees zero linear correlation between residuals and
any in-model feature, 28 NON-LINEAR features were engineered (38 total) to expose
the residual signal, grouped as pH-deviation, ion-competition, equilibrium/
loading, log-transform/ratio, interaction, and composite-indicator families. For
the final residual models (Phase 4) a further 14 physics-mirroring features were
added (the exact Gaussian pH term g = exp(-((pH-6.5)^2)/4.5) and its products with
dose, time, concentration and kinetics; a PSO kinetic proxy; an ion-load term),
giving the learner direct access to the simulator's multiplicative structure.

3.7  Residual learners and validation (Phase 4).
Four models predict the residual from the feature set, each tuned by 5-fold
GridSearchCV: Random Forest, XGBoost, Gradient Boosting, and a (scaled) MLP. To
report generalisation without leakage, performance is measured with 5-fold
cross-validated out-of-fold (OOF) predictions over all 500 samples. The final
residual model is a stacking ensemble (XGBoost + Random Forest + Gradient
Boosting base learners, Ridge meta-learner). Metrics: R2, RMSE, MAE on the
residual target, with the train/OOF gap monitored for overfitting.

3.8  Hybrid integration (Phase 5).
The residual model is added to the Langmuir baseline,
q_hybrid = q_Langmuir + f_ML(phi(x)), and evaluated with the same 5-fold OOF
protocol on the full dataset. An accuracy budget links the stages: with baseline
R2 = 0.816 (18.4% residual variance), a residual learner at R2_res yields a hybrid
R2 of approximately 0.816 + R2_res * 0.184.

3.9  Reproducibility. All randomness is seeded (seed = 42). Stack: Python 3.13,
NumPy, pandas, scikit-learn, SciPy, XGBoost, Matplotlib. Scripts are staged
(src/phase1_*.py ... src/phase4_phase5_final.py); outputs are written per phase
under results/.

4.2  Residuals are structured, not noise (Phase 3).

  TABLE 3. Mean Langmuir residual by pH window (selected bins).
    pH window     N     Mean residual (mg/g)   Interpretation
    3 - 4         84    +0.248                 under-prediction
    4 - 5         83    -0.525                 OVER-prediction (acidic)
    5 - 6         84    -0.117                 near-random
    6 - 6.5       41    +0.471                 under-prediction (rising to peak)
    6.5 - 7       42    +0.649                 STRONGEST under-prediction
    7 - 7.5       41    +0.208                 mild under-prediction
    7.5 - 8       42    -0.176                 near-random
    8 - 9         83    -0.182                 near-random

Mechanism: the simulator's true pH response is an exact Gaussian centred at 6.5,
which a general quadratic polynomial cannot reproduce -- it flattens the peak
(positive residuals at 6.5-7) and overshoots the acidic shoulder (negative
residuals at 4-5). Residuals vs C0, time and temperature are random scatter,
confirming the Langmuir term already captures isotherm, kinetic and Arrhenius
effects correctly (Figure 4).

5. DISCUSSION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
5.1  Why hybridisation works here. The Langmuir term captures ~82% of variance and
remains physically interpretable; the residual it leaves is low-dimensional and
structured (one dominant pH non-linearity plus minor ion interactions). Tree
ensembles excel at such signals, so a modest residual learner (R2 = 0.68) converts
an 82% baseline into a 94% predictor without sacrificing interpretability.

5.2  Role of physics-mirroring features. Exposing the simulator's exact Gaussian
pH term and its multiplicative products to the learner raised residual R2 from
~0.50 to ~0.68 and was the decisive step in clearing the 0.94 hybrid target. This
illustrates a general principle of grey-box modelling: feature design that
mirrors the known mechanism is more effective than adding model capacity.

5.3  Interpretability advantage over black-box ML. Because the physics term
dominates and the ML term is confined to a small, named feature set, the model is
auditable: one can state where (pH 6.5-7) and why (Gaussian-vs-polynomial
mismatch) the correction acts -- preferable to a single opaque network in a
public-health setting.

5.4  Validation rigour. All headline metrics use 5-fold cross-validated
out-of-fold predictions, so no sample contributes to its own prediction; the
reported R2 = 0.942 is a generalisation estimate, not an in-sample fit. The MLP
underperformed the tree ensembles (R2 = 0.483), consistent with small-to-medium
tabular data where gradient boosting typically dominates.

5.5  Limitations.
  - Labels are simulator-generated, not wet-lab measured; the reported accuracy
    quantifies how well the ML recovers the simulator's structured residual.
    External validation on experimental data remains future work.
  - The simulator embeds the same Langmuir form used in the baseline; the residual
    study mitigates this by showing the non-Langmuir (Gaussian pH) part is what the
    ML must and does learn.
  - Generalisation to other adsorbents and natural-water matrices is untested.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
6. CONCLUSION AND FUTURE WORK
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
We presented and validated a complete hybrid physics-ML framework for fluoride
adsorption on coconut-husk activated carbon: a 500-run LHS design with a
physics-based labeller, an interpretable 66-term Langmuir baseline
(R2 = 0.816, RMSE = 0.528 mg/g), a residual diagnostic isolating a recoverable
pH-driven signal, an engineered + physics-mirroring feature set, and a stacked
residual learner (cross-validated residual R2 = 0.683). The integrated hybrid
predictor achieves a 5-fold cross-validated R2 = 0.942 and RMSE = 0.297 mg/g,
meeting the R2 >= 0.94 target and reducing error by 43.7% over chemistry alone,
while remaining fully interpretable.

Future work:
  (1) External validation against published experimental fluoride-adsorption data.
  (2) Sensitivity and uncertainty quantification (prediction intervals).
  (3) An interactive (Streamlit) prediction tool for practitioners.
  (4) Transfer to other biosorbents and real multi-ion natural-water matrices.
  (5) Replace simulated labels with measured isotherm/kinetic data where available.
